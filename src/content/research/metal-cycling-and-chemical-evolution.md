# Metal Cycling and Chemical Evolution

Metals record the history of gas inflow, star formation, feedback, and recycling. This research direction studies how metallicity and abundance patterns are regulated by galaxy mass, size, gravitational potential, gas turbulence, and formation history.

## Key Questions

- What galaxy properties best predict gas-phase metallicity beyond stellar mass?
- Does gravitational potential regulate metal cycling in both local and high-redshift galaxies?
- How do radial metallicity gradients reveal the gas flows that drive disk evolution?
- What controls stellar abundance patterns in quenched galaxies?

## Approach

We use MaNGA integral-field spectroscopy, JWST/JADES high-redshift spectroscopy, and TNG50 simulations to compare metallicity relations across cosmic time. Strong-line metallicity diagnostics, spatially resolved profiles, and full-spectrum fitting are used to measure gas-phase metallicities, stellar populations, and element abundance patterns.

Observational results are interpreted together with simulations to separate the roles of stellar mass, size, star formation rate, velocity dispersion, turbulence, and gravitational potential.

## Recent Findings

- Galaxy size plays a key role in gas-phase metallicity, often outperforming star formation rate as a secondary predictor.
- At redshifts 1 to 7, compact galaxies tend to be more metal enriched at fixed stellar mass, suggesting potential-driven metal cycling toward cosmic dawn.
- Metallicity gradients support a picture in which coplanar gas inflow drives disk evolution.
- Local gas turbulence competes with radial inflow in shaping metallicity gradients.
- In quenched galaxies, central velocity dispersion is a primary driver of several stellar abundance ratios, including Mg, Na, C, and N relative to Fe.
