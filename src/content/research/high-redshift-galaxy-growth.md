# High-redshift Galaxy Growth

JWST has opened a direct view of low-mass and rapidly forming galaxies near cosmic dawn. This research direction studies how early galaxies build their stellar mass, produce ionizing photons, and change their sizes and internal structures over time.

## Key Questions

- How do galaxies grow in size and stellar mass at high redshift?
- When does galaxy growth transition from outside-in to inside-out?
- What internal structures promote efficient ionizing photon production and escape?
- Can in situ star formation alone explain the size evolution of galaxies beyond cosmic noon?

## Approach

We combine JWST/NIRCam, JWST/NIRSpec, and HST imaging to measure stellar masses, star formation rates, morphologies, radial surface-density profiles, and resolved spectral energy distributions. These measurements are used to reconstruct radial star formation histories and compare galaxy structure across redshift.

The studies also use JADES data to connect rest-frame wavelength-dependent sizes with short-timescale growth and to examine compact emission-line galaxies during the epoch of reionization.

## Recent Findings

- Star-forming galaxies are typically smaller at longer rest-frame wavelengths, linking color-dependent size measurements to recent growth.
- At high redshift, radial specific star formation profiles indicate that galaxies cannot grow in size through in situ star formation alone.
- At lower redshift, galaxies increasingly show inside-out growth.
- Galaxies at z around 7 often have compact, centrally concentrated, and bursty star formation.
- Central regions of reionization-era galaxies can show high ionizing photon production efficiencies and elevated escape fractions.
