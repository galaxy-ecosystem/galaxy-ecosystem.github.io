# Disk Formation and Halo Assembly

Galaxy disks form through the continuous exchange of gas, stars, metals, and angular momentum. This research direction connects disk assembly with dark matter halo growth, using both resolved observations and simulations to understand how galaxies acquire and redistribute material.

## Key Questions

- What physical processes set the sizes and star formation profiles of disk galaxies?
- How does coplanar gas inflow shape metallicity gradients and disk evolution?
- Can the Milky Way be used as a time-resolved laboratory for testing disk assembly models?
- How can halo assembly history be inferred from observable galaxy-group properties?

## Approach

This work combines Milky Way stellar abundances, MaNGA spatially resolved spectroscopy, SDSS galaxy groups, and TNG50 simulations. We compare observed profiles of star formation, metallicity, and stellar populations with models of gas inflow, outflow, radial migration, and angular-momentum evolution.

Machine-learning models trained on mock galaxy catalogs are also used to estimate halo assembly histories for observed SDSS galaxy groups.

## Recent Findings

- The recent chemical evolution of the Milky Way is consistent with a leaky accretion disk model.
- Coplanar inflow provides a natural explanation for observed gas-phase metallicity gradients.
- TNG50 disks show strong radial migration and large disk sizes, highlighting tensions between simulations and observed disk structure.
- Halo assembly history can be estimated from observable group properties when star-forming and quenched central galaxies are modeled separately.
- Combining halo mass and halo assembly time enables stronger observational tests of the halo-galaxy connection.
