# Multiphase Circumgalactic Medium

We study the circumgalactic medium (CGM) as the interface where galaxies exchange gas, metals, and energy with their surrounding dark matter halos. The papers in this direction use absorption lines in stacked spectra of background sources to trace gas phases that are otherwise difficult to observe directly.

## Key Questions

- How are cold, cool, and warm gas phases distributed around galaxies and quasars?
- How do CGM absorption profiles depend on stellar mass, star formation rate, impact parameter, and azimuthal angle?
- What roles do stellar feedback and AGN feedback play in shaping the radial structure of the CGM?
- How do metals move from galaxies into the CGM and larger-scale environments over cosmic time?

## Approach

This work combines large spectroscopic samples from DESI with stacking analyses of background quasars and galaxies. Multiple absorption tracers, including Ca II, Mg II, C IV, Ly-alpha, Fe II, and other metal lines, are used to separate gas phases across a broad temperature range.

The analysis connects observed equivalent-width profiles with host-galaxy properties and compares the inferred phase structure with predictions from cosmological simulations and halo models.

## Recent Findings

- Cooler CGM phases are more centrally concentrated than warmer phases.
- Mg II absorption around galaxies depends strongly on stellar mass and star formation activity.
- Star-forming galaxies show enhanced Mg II absorption near the minor axis, consistent with outflow-driven cool gas.
- Quasar-associated multiphase gas shows distinct spatial and redshift evolution for low- and high-ionization species.
- Multiphase absorption measurements provide direct constraints on the baryon cycle connecting galaxies, halos, and the intergalactic medium.
