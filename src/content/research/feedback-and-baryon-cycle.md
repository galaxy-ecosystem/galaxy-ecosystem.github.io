# Feedback and the Baryon Cycle

Galactic feedback regulates how galaxies grow by launching gas out of star-forming regions, enriching the CGM, and changing the baryon content of dark matter halos. This research direction focuses on outflows traced by Mg II absorption and on the connection between feedback, escape velocity, and baryon retention.

## Key Questions

- How common are cool gas outflows across stellar mass and redshift?
- Can outflows escape from galaxies, or do they return as galactic fountains?
- Why do low-mass halos retain fewer baryons than expected from the cosmic baryon fraction?
- Does the relation between stellar mass and outflow strength evolve from cosmic noon to cosmic dawn?

## Approach

We use statistical stacking of galaxy spectra and background-source absorption measurements to detect weak Mg II outflow signatures in large samples. By comparing outflow velocities with halo escape velocities, we test whether gas is likely to escape the halo or recycle back into the galaxy.

The work spans nearby galaxies, DESI samples, and public JWST/NIRSpec spectra at high redshift, allowing the feedback cycle to be studied across a long cosmic timeline.

## Recent Findings

- Mg II-traced cool gas outflows are detected statistically from intermediate redshift to the early Universe.
- Outflow strength correlates positively with stellar mass across multiple epochs.
- Many high-redshift outflows remain below the halo escape velocity, supporting a fountain-like recycling picture.
- In low-mass galaxies, stellar-feedback-driven outflows may escape beyond the dark matter halo.
- Escaping cool gas provides observational evidence that feedback contributes to the baryon deficiency of low-mass halos.
